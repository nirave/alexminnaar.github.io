Word2Vec Tutorial Part III: Techniques for Computational Efficiency

  • by Alex Minnaar
  • on Mon 18 May 2015

Category: NLP Supervised Learning

In the previous two posts we saw that both the skip-gram and continous bag-of-words Word2Vec models are powerful tools for encoding a word's semantic information. However, in both of these models, computing \(\mathbf{v'}_w\) is computationally expensive in their current forms. This is because each training instance requires us to compute the prediction error

$$\frac{\partial{E}}{\partial{u_{c,j}}}=y_{c,j} - t_{c,j}$$

and to compute \(y_{c,j}\) (for the skip-gram model) we must evaluate

$$y_{c,j}=\frac{exp(u_{c,j})}{\sum_{j'=1}^V exp(u_{j'})}$$

which requires us to compute \(u_{j'}\) for every word in the vocabulary \(V\). In practice, there are usually tens of thousands of words in a typical vocabulary and several million training instances are usually required to produce a good Word2Vec model. Clearly this is computationally prohibitive. Fortunately, there are a couple of techniques that can be applied to Word2Vec models to make them more computationally efficient - hierarchical softmax and negative subsampling.
